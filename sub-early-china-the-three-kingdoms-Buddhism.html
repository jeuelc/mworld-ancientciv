Buddhism

According to legend, Buddhism was first introduced in 65 CE when Emperor Mingdi invited two monks to the Han capital after he had a dream. At first, only educated people in the cities were interested in this new religion. But eventually Buddhism became more popular and started influencing established local religions like Daoism.

Buddhism offered an optimistic view of life after death in a time of political uncertainty like the one following the collapse of the Han Dynasty. With wars and famine raging across the land as warlords squabbled for power, people flocked to this new religion and ushered in what would become known as the 'golden age' of Chinese Buddhism.

In the north, the new Hun rulers and other nomad groups came to value Buddhism and Buddhists. The Huns didn't trust Confucian officials, who seemed obsessed with formalities and loudly disapproved of foreign 'barbarians' ruling Chinese people. Therefore the Hun rulers decided to rely on limited groups of educated people such as Buddhist monks and Daoists to run their new empire.

Daoism

Daoism (also called 'Taoism') is an old religion practised by many millions of Chinese. In fact, if you've ever seen people performing tai chi in a park, you've seen Daoism in action! The Dao literally means 'the way' or 'the path', and emphasises harmony with the world. Followers are expected to avoid aggression and balance others' hatred with passivity.
