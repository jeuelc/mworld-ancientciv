Schooling

 

As Roman boys grew up, their mothers would spend money and time to advance their political careers. Girls would receive this sort of home education too because they would be expected to teach their own children one day.

Hence, education started at home. In the Early Roman Republic there were no public schools. In the third and second centuries BCE, very wealthy people provided Greek tutors for their children at home. For the less wealthy there were private schools where Greek-educated slaves taught.

The clever Roman students often continued their studies in prestigious schools of oratory (public speaking). Public speaking was crucial for a career as a lawyer and/or politician.

A final level of education was philosophical study, and students often went to Greece for this. The most important philosophy in Rome was 'Stoicism', which originated in Hellenistic Greece. Stoic teachings were first carried to Rome in 155 BCE by Diogenes of Babylon. Stoic principles urged the Romans to avoid all emotional excess and dedicate themselves to public good through political activity.

Hellenistic Greece

Hellenistic Greece refers to a period between the death of Alexander the Great in 323 BC and the emergence of the Roman Empire 31 BC.
