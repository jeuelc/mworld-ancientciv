Roman Theatre

Theatre in Rome flourished from the third century BCE after the Romans came in contact with the Greeks.

Comedy was very popular in early Rome, especially as all the funny characters had Greek names! Plautus and Terence were the most famous comic playwrights and they often translated whole Greek comedies, particularly those of Menander, into Latin.

In their plays they use stock characters such as the clever slave, the stingy old man, the arrogant soldier. Because the Romans had no theatrical education, the first theatres were wooden, temporary structures. Pompey the Great built a magnificent permanent theatre in Rome only in 55 BCE.

Unlike the Greeks, the Romans did not like tragedies! Only the philosopher Seneca wrote some tragedies in the first century CE. They were frightening plays! They were full of violence and designed to criticize the decadent life of the Emperor Nero.
