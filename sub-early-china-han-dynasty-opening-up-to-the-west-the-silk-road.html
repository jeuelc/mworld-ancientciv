Opening up to the West - the Silk Road

The Han were responsible for opening up the Silk Road, a long network of trade routes between Europe, India and China. This involved a lot of long, very violent military campaigns against a nomadic people known in China as the Xiongnu and in Europe as the Huns.

In the 2nd century BCE, a Han envoy named Zhang Qian spent many years travelling through Central Asia, including ten years as a hostage after he was caught by the Huns, in areas that would later become part of the Silk Road network. On his second expedition to Central Asia, Zhang Qian was able to secure a good herd of superb horses to fight the nomads.

After almost a century of occasional warfare and fighting, the Han eventually managed to chase the Huns out of western China. The Silk Road was now open for business! Trade flourished between Asia and Europe, and the Han Empire made fabulous profits from taxing traders and merchants who did business along the Silk Road.
