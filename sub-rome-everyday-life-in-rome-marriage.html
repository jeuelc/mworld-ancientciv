Marriage

 

Marriage in ancient Rome was arranged between families. The bridal dowry was agreed and the father of the bride would sign the wedding contract. Divorce was introduced in the second century BCE and was fairly easy to obtain. The husband was required by law to refer any alleged misconduct of his wife to the 'idicium domesticum' (civil court).

Some of the marriage customs you see today started in ancient Rome: for example, the bride wore a long white robe and a bridal veil (but bright yellow shoes!). She was escorted in the evening to her future husband's home. During the march, rude songs called the 'versus Fescennini' were sung! The groom carried the bride over the threshold.

Wives had a place at public games, at theatres, and at great religious ceremonies. They could testify in court and, until late in the Republic, might even defend a case! The Roman festival of 'Matronalia' (festival of women) was celebrated on the kalends the first day of March.

Dowry

A dowry is the goods and possessions a woman's family gives to her bridegroom, and his family, when she marries.
