Roman Slavery

 

Like the Greeks, the Romans relied on slaves to do the physical, back-breaking labour required for running their households, shops and farms. The slave trade flourished in antiquity and in 146 BCE, when Rome defeated Carthage, 50,000 people were sold into slavery. The conditions for slaves were generally bad and although the law protected them, their masters had more or less absolute power over them.

In 73 BCE, a Thracian slave named Spartacus led a revolt of 70,000 slaves in Sicily before they were crushed by the Roman army. Slaves, especially cooks, had a terrible reputation as thieves and often featured in Roman comedy.

Educated Greek slaves could often become their masters' friends and buy their freedom (this is called manumission). One famous example of this was Cicero and his freedman ('libertus') Tiro.
