Despite the wars and chaos and banditry of the Three Kingdoms period, long-distance trade continued in the north along the Silk Road and along the southern sea routes. People still wanted to make money and indulge themselves with foreign luxuries!

Just as during the Han Dynasty, China remained in contact with its neighbours particularly Korea and Vietnam as well as distant places like India, Persia and Rome. While merchants traded gold and goods, the artists and monks who travelled with them exchanged ideas and techniques with their foreign counterparts. Buddhism was increasingly popular, so there was a brisk trade in translations (and translators!) of Buddhist religious texts, which were written in Sanskrit.

Goods such as glass, bronze and silver wares were traded across long distances, including all the way from the Mediterranean! For example, traders brought sophisticated glassware from Rome to China. At first, this material was very exclusive and expensive. However, competing traders, from the region known as Sogdia (Tajikistan) developed other ways to make glass and the price collapsed. Even in ancient times competition could make business difficult!
