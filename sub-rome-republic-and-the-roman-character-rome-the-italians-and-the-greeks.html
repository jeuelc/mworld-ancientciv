Rome, the Italians and the Greeks

After expelling the Etruscans, the Romans turned their attention to the rest of Italy. Conquest became their way of ensuring safety 'get them before they get us'!

In 387 BCE their strategy was dramatically proven right: the Gauls attacked and burned Rome to the ground. It took the Romans nearly 40 years to rebuild their city.

As soon as they did, they set about conquering all peoples in the Italian peninsula. They fought wars with the Samnites and eventually defeated them in 295 BCE, gaining control of all of central Italy. They then took over the Greek cities in the south of Italy and Sicily. Although the Greeks were conquered by the Romans, they influenced the Roman way of life irreversibly.

Stern Romans such as Cato the Elder were always suspicious of the Greeks' love for freedom and the arts, while others, such as the progressive Lucius Aemilius Macedonicus, admired the Greeks endlessly and supported the introduction of Greek literature and education in Rome.
