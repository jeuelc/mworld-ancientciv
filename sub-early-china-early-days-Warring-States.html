There was a time in China when it seemed like everyone was always fighting! That's why it became known as the 'Warring States' period.

About 2500 years ago, in the middle Zhou period, states competed for political and economic control, and this resulted in fewer and larger kingdoms. Chariot warfare was common at first, gradually being replaced by more efficient infantry and cavalry. But chariots remained a symbol of the elites and chariot burials and human sacrifices continued throughout the Warring States period.

During this time urban life was further developed. Metal-making was improved with iron technology used for weapons and utensils. People starting using coins, which helped expand a market economy that was also benefiting from a network of roads and riverways. The early segments of the Great Wall were also built at this time, to protect against recurring raids by northern nomads.

Despite the constant warfare, this was one of the most prolific periods in early China in terms of cultural, philosophical and artistic developments.
