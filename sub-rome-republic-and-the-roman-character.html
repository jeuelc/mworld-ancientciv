Republic and the Roman character

Early Rome was governed by a monarch, who had absolute control over the people. The Romans called this power 'imperium'. There was also the Senate and the assembly. The Senate was a council of elders and the assembly was composed of all male citizens from 30 Roman kinship groups called 'curiae'. Each 'curia' got a single vote, so the assembly had a total of 30 votes.

From early on though, Roman society was divided into two groups, the wealthy patricians and the poor 'plebeians'. Only the wealthy could take part in government. The poor were represented by the assembly.

After the Etruscan kings were expelled from Rome, the Romans decided they would have a republic the 'Res Publica'. Instead of a monarch, the Senate now elected two men called 'consuls' from among the patricians each year. But the consuls exercised 'imperium' in much the same way the kings had. They even dressed as monarchs, wearing purple robes and sitting on the ivory chair traditionally reserved for the monarch!
