The Senate

 

The Senate was the main assembly of the Roman Republic. The people in it were those who had previously been elected in magisterial positions. It passed decrees called 'senatus consulta', which were formal recommendations to the various magistrates and especially the two consuls.

The Senate took its authority from tradition there was no actual law that said 'You are the boss!' But the Senate could control the military affairs of the city and when Rome expanded its power, it also supervised how the provinces were run. Importantly, it was the Senate which appointed a dictator, that is, a governor entrusted with special powers to deal with urgent political crises. Later, the Senate gave these special powers to the consuls instead. The place where the Senate met was called the 'Curia' and the first one was built by the legendary king Tullus Hostilius in the mid-7th century BCE.

Under the Roman Empire, the Senate still operated, but it was controlled by the emperor. Nowadays, the term Senate is used by the American, British, and Australian constitutions with varying meanings.
